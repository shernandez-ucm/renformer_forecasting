% Abstract
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{abstract}
Time-series foundation models (TSFMs) such as TimesFM promise accurate
zero-shot forecasting from a single large pre-trained network, and recent
studies report that they outperform classical baselines even in
specialised domains. We ask whether this scale is necessary when abundant
domain data is available. We present \textbf{REnFormer}, a compact
domain-specific Transformer (536\,k parameters) for probabilistic
multi-site solar power forecasting, trained on 291 photovoltaic plants
from Chile's national electricity system (SEN) covering 5.4 years of
hourly generation. REnFormer combines per-window reversible instance
normalisation---mirroring the input scaling of TSFMs---with a shared
encoder, a macro skip connection, and a Gaussian output head trained with
a \emph{masked negative log-likelihood} that concentrates learning on
non-trivial generation steps. In a like-for-like evaluation against
TimesFM~2.5 (231M parameters, zero-shot) over fleet sizes from 10 to 291
sites, REnFormer matches the foundation model on MAE at full fleet scale
(1.936 vs.\ 1.936\,MW) and improves RMSE by 3.2\% and CRPS by 5.1\%,
while using $431\times$ fewer parameters and running $\sim$640$\times$
faster per forecast (0.037\,ms vs.\ 24\,ms). The probabilistic advantage
holds at \emph{every} fleet size evaluated. Our results add domain-specific
evidence to a growing literature suggesting that small, specialised
forecasting models remain a Pareto-optimal choice when in-domain history
exists, relegating TSFMs to cold-start and data-scarce regimes.
\end{abstract}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

Time-series forecasting is undergoing the same scale-driven transition
that reshaped natural language processing: large pre-trained
\emph{time-series foundation models} (TSFMs) such as
TimesFM~\cite{das2024timesfm} offer zero-shot forecasts from a single
network with hundreds of millions of parameters, and LLM-based
forecasters repurpose billion-parameter language backbones for numeric
sequences~\cite{zhang2024survey,liu2024autotimes,niu2025langtime}.
Recent evaluations report that TSFMs beat classical econometric
baselines out of the box, even in specialised domains such as
macroeconomic forecasting~\cite{katharakis2025slm}.

A growing counter-literature, however, questions whether this scale is
doing the work. Ablation studies show that removing the pre-trained LLM
from LLM-based forecasters often does not hurt---and sometimes
improves---accuracy~\cite{tan2024useful}. Sub-3B ``small language
model'' forecasters match or exceed 7B baselines at a fraction of the
training cost~\cite{fan2025smetimes}; compact architectures distil the
inductive biases of large vision-based forecasters into models three
orders of magnitude smaller~\cite{shen2025svtime}; and recent
lightweight foundation models (23M--100M parameters) trained on modest
hardware reach state-of-the-art benchmark
performance~\cite{birkel2025tinytsm,he2025sempo}. The emerging picture
is that for time series---unlike text---parameter count is a poor proxy
for forecasting skill, and that small, well-designed models are
competitive whenever relevant training data exists.

This paper provides a controlled, operationally grounded data point for
that debate in the renewable energy domain. Solar power forecasting at
grid scale is an ideal test bed: national system operators hold years of
hourly generation history for every plant (so the data-scarcity argument
for TSFMs does not apply), the fleet is large and heterogeneous (so
per-site models do not scale), and forecasts must be produced for
hundreds of sites every hour (so inference cost matters). Solar
generation is also structurally hard for generic pre-trained models: it
is exactly zero for roughly half of all timesteps and highly
intermittent otherwise, a regime that generic training corpora
under-represent.

We present \textbf{REnFormer} (\textbf{R}enewable \textbf{En}ergy
Trans\textbf{Former}), a domain-specific Transformer with 536\,k
parameters---0.23\% the size of TimesFM~2.5---trained globally on 291
solar plants of Chile's national electricity system (SEN). Our
contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A \textbf{compact global architecture}: a shared 4-layer encoder
    with per-window reversible instance normalisation (the same input
    scaling principle TSFMs rely on), a macro skip connection, and a
    probabilistic Gaussian head, totalling 536\,k parameters.
  \item A \textbf{masked Gaussian NLL objective} that focuses learning
    on non-trivial generation steps ($>0.1$\,MW), directly addressing
    the intermittency structure that generic foundation models must
    handle implicitly.
  \item A \textbf{like-for-like comparison against TimesFM~2.5
    zero-shot} on identical test windows across fleet sizes from 10 to
    291 sites. At full fleet scale REnFormer matches the foundation
    model on MAE and improves RMSE (3.2\%) and CRPS (5.1\%), at
    $431\times$ fewer parameters and $\sim$640$\times$ lower inference
    latency; its probabilistic advantage holds at every fleet size.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Foundation Models and LLMs for Time Series}

TimesFM~\cite{das2024timesfm} established the decoder-only pre-trained
foundation-model recipe for forecasting; a parallel line adapts language
models directly, surveyed in~\cite{zhang2024survey}.
AutoTimes~\cite{liu2024autotimes} repurposes frozen decoder-only LLMs
autoregressively with only 0.1\% trainable parameters, and
LangTime~\cite{niu2025langtime} adds cross-domain pre-training and
reinforcement-learning fine-tuning to stabilise autoregressive rollout.
Foundation models can also be adapted cheaply at inference time:
TFMAdapter~\cite{dange2025tfmadapter} injects covariates into frozen
TSFM forecasts with a lightweight instance-level adapter, gaining
24--27\% over the base models. In application studies, zero-shot TSFMs
(TimesFM, Moirai) outperformed econometric baselines for macroeconomic
forecasting across 27 EU countries~\cite{katharakis2025slm}---evidence
that motivates our choice of TimesFM~2.5 as the strongest available
zero-shot comparator.

\subsection{Small and Lightweight Forecasting Models}

The necessity of scale is increasingly contested. Tan et
al.~\cite{tan2024useful} show through ablation that the pre-trained LLM
component of several prominent LLM-forecasters can be removed without
loss of accuracy, attributing reported gains to the surrounding
architecture. SMETimes~\cite{fan2025smetimes} demonstrates that sub-3B
models with statistically enhanced prompts and a mixture-of-experts
outperform 7B baselines with far less compute.
SVTime~\cite{shen2025svtime} encodes the inductive biases of large
vision-model forecasters into a small model with $10^3\times$ fewer
parameters, and Tiny-TSM~\cite{birkel2025tinytsm} trains a 23M-parameter
foundation model to state-of-the-art medium/long-horizon accuracy on a
single GPU. SEMPO~\cite{he2025sempo} reaches strong zero- and few-shot
performance with a small footprint via spectral decomposition and
prompt mixtures. These works compress or replace \emph{general-purpose}
models; REnFormer is complementary, asking how far a small model goes
when it is \emph{specialised}: trained from scratch on a single domain
with abundant history, and equipped with domain-aware components
(intermittency masking, per-window renormalisation).

\subsection{Multi-Site PV Forecasting}

Global models---one parameterisation shared across all
series~\cite{torres2019bigdata}---have gained traction for PV fleets:
Jang et al.~\cite{jang2024multisite} adapt a shared model to known and
unseen Korean sites, and Dimitriadis et
al.~\cite{dimitriadis2025multicountry} scale to three interconnected
European countries using input normalisation against cross-border
distribution shift. Transformer architectures dominate recent work: the
Temporal Fusion Transformer for day-ahead PV~\cite{lopezSantos2022tft},
inter-series attention for sparse demand~\cite{cristian2024interseries},
and Transformers for intermittent EV load~\cite{koohfar2023ev}.
Spatial-temporal GNNs~\cite{bai2023graphgru,yang2025destgnn,bai2025dma}
model inter-site dependencies explicitly but require a pre-defined
adjacency structure and $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ edge computation. REnFormer
operates in the purely global regime---no per-site parameters, no
graph---which is what allows a single 536\,k-parameter network to serve
an entire national fleet.

\section{Problem Formulation}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

Let $\mathcal{S} = \{1, \ldots, N\}$ denote a fleet of $N = 291$ solar
plants. For each plant $s \in \mathcal{S}$ and time $t$, let $y_{s,t} \in
\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ denote the hourly generation in MW.

\textbf{Global multi-site forecasting.}
Given a lookback window of $T$ consecutive hours,
$\mathbf{x}_s = (y_{s,t-T+1}, \ldots, y_{s,t}) \in \mathbb{R}^T$,
predict the next $H$ hourly values
$\mathbf{y}_s = (y_{s,t+1}, \ldots, y_{s,t+H}) \in \mathbb{R}^H$
using a single shared model $f_\theta$ such that:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{y}}_s = f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_s), \quad \forall s \in \mathcal{S}.
\end{equation}
The model parameters $\theta$ are shared across all sites; sites
contribute independently as training instances, exactly as series do for
a zero-shot foundation model. This makes the comparison with
TimesFM~2.5 like-for-like: both models map a raw-MW history window to a
forecast, relying on internal per-window normalisation to handle the
three-orders-of-magnitude spread in plant capacity.

\textbf{Intermittency mask.}
Define a binary mask $\mathbf{m}_s \in \{0,1\}^H$ with
$m_{s,t+h} = \mathbf{1}[y_{s,t+h} > \epsilon]$
for a threshold $\epsilon > 0$ (we set $\epsilon = 0.1$\,MW). Masked
timesteps correspond to trivially near-zero generation steps (nighttime,
zero-irradiance) where prediction accuracy is operationally irrelevant.

\textbf{Probabilistic objective.}
$f_\theta$ outputs Gaussian parameters $(\boldsymbol{\mu}_s,
\boldsymbol{\sigma}_s^2) \in \mathbb{R}^H \times \mathbb{R}^H_{>0}$.
The training objective is the masked negative log-likelihood (NLL):
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{B}}
  \frac{\sum_{h=1}^{H} m_{s,t+h}\, \log \mathcal{N}(y_{s,t+h};
    \mu_{s,h}, \sigma_{s,h}^2)}{\sum_{h=1}^{H} m_{s,t+h} + \delta},
  \label{eq:masked_nll}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{B}$ is a mini-batch and $\delta = 10^{-6}$ prevents
division by zero on all-dark batches.

REnFormer is deliberately small: the design question is not how much
capacity can be added, but which components a compact model needs to
absorb a heterogeneous national fleet.

\subsection{Per-Window Reversible Normalisation}

The model receives \emph{raw MW} input. Each lookback window's power
channel is normalised by its own statistics before encoding, and the
Gaussian outputs are mapped back to MW by the inverse transform---the
reversible instance normalisation (RevIN) principle~\cite{kim2022revin},
and the same mechanism that lets TimesFM ingest unscaled
series. This preserves per-site capacity information in the window
statistics while letting a single encoder serve plants from $<1$\,MW
rooftops to $\sim$390\,MW utility installations. The base variant uses
RevIN with learnable affine parameters; the skip variant (below) uses
plain per-window mean/std normalisation. Exogenous calendar channels
(sin/cos hour and day-of-year) bypass normalisation.

\subsection{Input Encoding}

A learned linear projection maps each timestep from $\mathbb{R}^F$ to
$\mathbb{R}^{d}$ ($F$ input channels, model dimension $d$):
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_t^{(0)} = \mathbf{W}_{\text{in}}\, \mathbf{x}_{s,t} + \mathbf{b}_{\text{in}},
  \quad \mathbf{W}_{\text{in}} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times F}.
\end{equation}
Sinusoidal positional encodings~\cite{vaswani2017attention} are then added:
\begin{equation}
  \text{PE}_{(t,2k)} = \sin\!\left(\frac{t}{10000^{2k/d}}\right), \quad
  \text{PE}_{(t,2k+1)} = \cos\!\left(\frac{t}{10000^{2k/d}}\right).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Transformer Encoder with Macro Skip Connection}

The encoder consists of $L$ identical post-LayerNorm Transformer blocks,
each applying multi-head self-attention (MHSA) followed by a
position-wise feed-forward network (FFN) with GELU:
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{a}^{(\ell)} &= \text{MHSA}\!\left(\mathbf{H}^{(\ell-1)}\right),\\
  \mathbf{H}^{(\ell)} &= \text{LayerNorm}\!\left(\mathbf{H}^{(\ell-1)}
    + \mathbf{a}^{(\ell)}\right),\\
  \mathbf{H}^{(\ell)} &= \text{LayerNorm}\!\left(\mathbf{H}^{(\ell)}
    + \text{FFN}\!\left(\mathbf{H}^{(\ell)}\right)\right).
\end{align}
The skip variant (\textbf{REnFormer-Skip}, used in the foundation-model
comparison) additionally stores the projected, position-encoded input
$\mathbf{H}^{(0)}$ and re-injects it after the final block:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{H}^{\text{out}} = \text{LayerNorm}\!\left(\mathbf{H}^{(L)}
    + \mathbf{H}^{(0)}\right),
\end{equation}
a macro residual that gives the head direct access to the normalised
input pattern---useful for a strongly diurnal signal whose next day
resembles a scaled copy of recent days. With $T = 168$ (7-day lookback)
and $d = 128$, the $\mathcal{O}(T^2 d)$ attention cost is negligible on
a single GPU.

\subsection{Probabilistic Forecast Head}

The encoded representation of the last token,
$\mathbf{h}^{(L)}_T \in \mathbb{R}^d$, is pooled as a summary of the
full context. Two parallel linear layers project this to the forecast
horizon:
\begin{align}
  \boldsymbol{\mu} &= \mathbf{W}_\mu \, \mathbf{h}^{(L)}_T \in \mathbb{R}^H,\\
  \log \boldsymbol{\sigma} &= \mathbf{W}_\sigma \, \mathbf{h}^{(L)}_T \in \mathbb{R}^H,\\
  \boldsymbol{\sigma} &= \exp(\log \boldsymbol{\sigma}).
\end{align}
The log-space parametrisation guarantees $\sigma_h > 0$. During
inference, the point forecast is $\hat{y}_h = \mu_h$ and a 90\%
prediction interval is $[\mu_h - 1.645\sigma_h,\, \mu_h +
1.645\sigma_h]$, all de-normalised to MW.

\subsection{Training}

REnFormer is trained end-to-end by minimising the masked NLL in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:masked_nll} using Adam with learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$,
cosine annealing, and a batch size of 64 windows. Supervision is in raw
MW (the normalisation is inverted inside the forward pass), so the loss
directly reflects operational error. Each window is drawn uniformly at
random from all sites and dates; sites receive no explicit identifier,
forcing the model to learn site-invariant temporal patterns.

\section{Experiments}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Dataset: SEN Chile}

We evaluate on the \emph{Descarga Generaci\'on Real} dataset published by
Chile's National Energy Coordinator (CEN), covering the national
electricity system (SEN) from 2021-01-01 to 2026-05-29. The raw export
contains 817 grid-connected solar units; after removing sites with more
than 10\% missing hours, the corpus contains:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{291 solar plants} spanning the Atacama Desert to the
    south of Chile, with capacities from $<1$\,MW to $\sim$390\,MW
  \item \textbf{47,400 hours} (5.4 years) of generation per site,
    $\approx$13.8 million site-hours in total
  \item \textbf{6.49 million} non-zero hourly observations
  \item Intermittent fraction: 57.9\% of timesteps with generation
    $\leq 0.1$\,MW (nighttime + overcast)
\end{itemize}

We apply a chronological split: training on 2021--2023 (26,280\,h),
validation on 2024 (8,784\,h), and test on 2025-01-01 to 2026-05-29
(12,336\,h), yielding 7.59M / 2.50M / 3.53M sliding windows
respectively. The test period is strictly future relative to training,
so newer plants and fleet growth are part of the evaluation, matching
deployment conditions.

\subsection{Comparison Protocol}

The central comparison is REnFormer-Skip against
\textbf{TimesFM~2.5} (200M-class checkpoint, 231,289,280 parameters)
used \emph{zero-shot}, the strongest available foundation-model
comparator for this task~\cite{das2024timesfm,katharakis2025slm}. Both
models receive identical inputs: the same 507 non-overlapping
168-hour raw-MW windows per site from the test period (stride 24\,h),
forecasting the next 24 hours. Neither model is given site identifiers
or meteorological covariates. To characterise behaviour as the fleet
grows, we sweep the number of sites $N \in \{10, 60, 110, 160, 210,
260, 291\}$ (sites in fixed order; 291 = full fleet).

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

For point forecasts we report mean absolute error (MAE) and root mean
squared error (RMSE) in MW, computed only on non-trivial timesteps
($m_{s,t} = 1$, i.e., generation $> 0.1$\,MW), to avoid inflating
accuracy with trivially correct nighttime zeros. For probabilistic
quality we report the \emph{continuous ranked probability score} (CRPS),
which jointly evaluates sharpness and calibration:
\begin{equation}
  \text{CRPS}(F, y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}
  \bigl(F(z) - \mathbf{1}[z \geq y]\bigr)^2 \, dz,
\end{equation}
where $F$ is the predicted CDF. We additionally report parameter count
and wall-clock inference latency per 24-hour forecast (ms/forecast) on
identical hardware.

\subsection{Main Results: Small Specialist vs.\ Foundation Model}

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} reports the full-fleet (291 sites)

latency. Forecasting the entire national fleet for the full 17-month

\section{Discussion}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{When does the specialist win?}
Our results delineate the regimes cleanly. With 5+ years of in-domain
history, a 536\,k-parameter specialist matches a 231M-parameter
foundation model on point accuracy and beats it on probabilistic
quality---consistent with findings that the architecture around the
forecaster, not pre-trained scale, drives accuracy~\cite{tan2024useful},
and with the success of compact models built with the right inductive
biases~\cite{shen2025svtime,birkel2025tinytsm}. The foundation model
retains a genuine advantage where the specialist cannot go: plants with
no history (cold-start) and grids without accumulated data. Lightweight
adaptation of TSFMs~\cite{dange2025tfmadapter} and few-shot compact
foundation models~\cite{he2025sempo} may eventually close the gap from
the other side; a hybrid that backs off to a TSFM for young plants is a
natural operational design.

\textbf{Operational cost.}
For a system operator issuing hourly forecasts for hundreds of plants,
the $\sim$640$\times$ latency gap is not cosmetic: it is the difference
between forecasting the fleet in seconds on commodity hardware and
dedicating GPU-hours per day, and it makes high-frequency re-forecasting
and large ensemble runs feasible. The full REnFormer training run
(50 epochs) completes on a single GPU, in line with the single-GPU
training budgets reported for other small
forecasters~\cite{fan2025smetimes,birkel2025tinytsm}.

\textbf{Why masking matters for solar.}
57.9\% of all site-hours are trivial ($\leq 0.1$\,MW). A generic
pre-trained model must spend capacity learning to reproduce zeros;
the masked NLL (Eq.~\ref{eq:masked_nll}) lets a small model spend its
entire 536\,k-parameter budget on the operationally relevant daylight
regime. We view this kind of domain-structure exploitation as the core
reason small specialists stay competitive.

\textbf{Limitations.}
REnFormer is trained without meteorological covariates (no NWP input),
relying solely on generation history; both models in our comparison are
therefore weather-blind, and adding irradiance forecasts would likely
benefit both. The TimesFM comparison is zero-shot: fine-tuning the
foundation model on SEN data would narrow the accuracy gap at the cost
of its main selling point (no training) while leaving the
431$\times$ parameter and inference gap intact. Finally, our fleet-size
sweep uses a fixed site ordering; metric levels across $N$ reflect the
mix of plant sizes added, not a pure scaling law.

\section{Conclusion}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

We presented REnFormer, a 536\,k-parameter domain-specific Transformer
for probabilistic multi-site solar forecasting, and evaluated it
like-for-like against the 231M-parameter TimesFM~2.5 foundation model
on 291 plants of the Chilean national grid. The small specialist matches
the foundation model's point accuracy at full fleet scale, improves
probabilistic skill at every fleet size evaluated, and runs
$\sim$640$\times$ faster per forecast. In a literature increasingly
sceptical that scale is what makes time-series models
work~\cite{tan2024useful,fan2025smetimes,birkel2025tinytsm}, our results
add grid-scale, operationally grounded evidence: when in-domain history
is abundant, a small Transformer with the right domain structure---
per-window reversible normalisation and intermittency-masked
